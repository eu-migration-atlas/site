Luxembourg

Luxembourg has a unique and dynamic migration landscape. With a population of about 660,000, nearly 47% of its inhabitants are foreign-born (the highest share in the OECD). Most foreign residents are EU citizens from Portugal, France, Belgium, and Germany, and more than 200,000 cross-border workers commute into the country every day. In 2024, around 26,000 new long-term immigrants were registered (61% EU nationals and 39% third-country nationals). Luxembourg also granted temporary protection to 3,940 displaced Ukrainians.

Europe at a glance

Luxembourg is one of the smallest EU Member States but has one of Europe’s most international populations. Its migration profile is shaped by high shares of foreign-born residents, strong intra-EU mobility, and large daily inflows of cross-border workers. Migration policy focuses on regulated legal migration (labour and family) and a structured asylum and reception system coordinated by the Office National de l’Accueil (ONA).

Political climate

Luxembourg’s politics are stable and typically centrist and pro-European. After the October 2023 elections, a coalition between the Christian Social People’s Party (CSV) and the liberal Democratic Party (DP) formed the government. Luc Frieden (CSV) became Prime Minister and Xavier Bettel (DP) became Deputy Prime Minister. Migration responsibilities are split across ministries: the Directorate of Immigration sits under the Ministry of the Interior, while social cohesion and migration integration are handled by the Ministry of Family Affairs. Parties broadly agree that regulated migration should continue, and the coalition supported the 2024 EU Migration and Asylum Pact (implementation planned from 2026), including EU-wide solidarity mechanisms and faster asylum procedures.

Migration statistics

Key figures from the profile: • Resident population composition (2024): Foreign-born 47.4%; EU nationals 32.9%; non-EU nationals 18.1%. • Top nationalities (2024): Portuguese 14.5%; French 7.6%; Italian 3.7%. • New long-term immigrants (2024): ~26,000 (61% EU nationals; 39% third-country nationals), including labour (12%), family reunification (22%), and humanitarian (4%). • First residence permits by purpose (2022): Family 45.2%; Work 34.7%; Study 6.8%; Protection 4.5%. • Asylum applications (2024): ~2,100 first-time applications; recognition rate 64%; main origins Eritrea and Syria. • Temporary protection for Ukrainians: 3,940 beneficiaries (extended under the EU directive until March 2026).

CP

Current policies

Luxembourg guarantees the right to asylum through national law and, since 2023, through its Constitution. ONA coordinates reception and accommodation and expanded capacity in response to rising demand, including the Ukrainian crisis. Since August 2023, labour market tests for asylum seekers were removed to facilitate access to employment and support faster socio-economic integration. Labour migration is demand-driven and based on the 2017 Immigration Law, amended in 2023 and 2024. Highly skilled migration uses the EU Blue Card, with an EU salary threshold of €58,968; since July 2024 Blue Card holders may move to another EU country after only 12 months of residence. Luxembourg also uses a national shortage occupation list and a fast-track procedure for priority professions. In 2023, efforts against illegal employment and trafficking were strengthened through higher penalties and enhanced inspections.

AP

Application possibilities

Reception and accommodation are organised through ONA, which expanded services in response to increased demand. Temporary protection includes a biometric residence card, housing, healthcare, and access to the job market, and has been extended until March 2026. Family reunification is a major part of legal immigration: in 2022, 45.2% of residence permits were for family reunification. Third-country nationals can apply if they have a residence permit valid for at least one year and meet minimum income and housing requirements. The state provides language and orientation support for reunited family members. Luxembourg also supports student retention by easing transitions from study permits to work visas.
